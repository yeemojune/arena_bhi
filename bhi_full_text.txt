[7] [Normal] 1 Large Language Models (LLMs) are advancing rapidly, yet the benchmarks used 2 to measure this progress are becoming increasingly unreliable. Score inflation 3 and selective reporting have eroded the authority of standard benchmarks, leaving 4 the community uncertain about which evaluation results remain trustworthy. We 5 introduce the Benchmark Health Index (BHI), a pure data-driven framework
[8] [Normal] for auditing evaluation sets along three orthogonal and complementary axes: (1)
[9] [Normal] Capability Discrimination, measuring how sharply a benchmark separates model 8	performance beyond noise; (2) Anti-Saturation, estimating remaining headroom
[10] [Normal] before ceiling effects erode resolution and thus the benchmark’s expected longevity;
[11] [Normal] and (3) Impact, measuring capability-weighted benchmark adoption across the
[12] [Normal] model ecosystem. By distilling 158 validated benchmarks from the technical re-
[13] [Normal] ports of 117 representative models released between 2025 and April 2026, we 13 systematically characterize the evaluation landscape. BHI is the first framework
[14] [Normal] to quantify benchmark health at a macro level, providing a well-grounded quan-
[15] [Normal] titative basis for benchmark selection and supporting the dynamic filtering and 16 continuous updating of evaluation sets. Our comprehensive analysis not only
[16] [Normal] identifies benchmarks that remain highly credible but also exposes pervasive struc-
[17] [Normal] tural issues. By establishing a rigorous foundation for selection and management, 19	BHI elevates evaluation practice from heuristic-based judgment to a quantifi20	able and interpretable scientific process. [38] [Normal] 48 Formalizing benchmark health faces three technical challenges: (1) Low Discrimination & Score 49 Compression: many benchmarks can no longer clearly separate strong models because their scores 50 are compressed near the top; (2) Ceiling Effect & Lack of Headroom: as benchmark data is 51 absorbed into training or repeatedly optimized for, the remaining challenge space shrinks, weakening 52 long-term progress tracking; and (3) Popularity Bias & Adoption Decoupling: frequent citation or 53 reporting does not necessarily mean that a benchmark is widely adopted by high-capability models 54 or remains technically informative. Together, these issues lead the community to overuse outdated 55 benchmarks and overlook more valuable ones.
[39] [Normal] To address this, we introduce Benchmark Health Index, a data-driven auditing framework that char-
[40] [Normal] acterizes benchmark validity and usability across three dimensions: (1) Capability Discrimination,
[41] [Normal] which measures whether a benchmark can clearly separate stronger models from weaker ones; (2)
[42] [Normal] Anti-Saturation, which evaluates whether a benchmark still remains challenging for current frontier
[43] [Normal] models and is unlikely to be exhausted soon; and (3) Impact, which measures whether a bench-
[44] [Normal] mark is widely adopted by high-capability models in real evaluation practice. By aggregating these, 62 BHI identifies effective benchmarks, demotes saturated tests to basic sanity checks, and designates 63 high-difficulty anchors for frontier capability tracking.
[45] [Normal] 64 We conducted a systematic meta-analysis of 158 benchmarks extracted from the technical reports of 65 117 representative LLMs released between 2025 and April 2026. Our study distinguishes effective 66 benchmarks that retain high discriminative power from those that, despite being widely used, have 67 become obsolete. Furthermore, we provide a systematic examination of benchmark distribution and 68 evolutionary trends across diverse task domains.
[46] [Normal] Our contributions are summarized as follows:
[47] [Normal] • We propose the BHI framework, which quantifies benchmark effectiveness and longevity 71	along three dimensions: Capability Discrimination, Anti-Saturation, and Impact.
[48] [Normal] 72 • We establish an objective, data-driven auditing protocol that implements a Leave-One73 Benchmark-Out calibration strategy and automated weighting mechanisms to eliminate 74 subjective bias and ensure methodological rigor.
[49] [Normal] 75 • Through large-scale meta-analysis and diagnostic case studies over 117 models and 158 76 benchmarks, we show that BHI separates saturated from frontier benchmarks, explains their 77 degradation patterns, and offers actionable guidance for benchmark governance.

[126] [Normal] 3.4	Impact (SImp)
[127] [Normal] SImp measures the ecological influence of a benchmark b ∈ B through its adoption by capable 170 models. Rather than relying on raw popularity signals such as repository stars, downloads, or 171 historical citation momentum, we define impact as capability-weighted evaluation coverage within 172 the model ecosystem.
[128] [Normal] 3.4.1	Capability-weighted Adoption
[129] [Normal] Let Mbench ⊆ Mall denote the set of models that have reported valid evaluation results on benchmark 175 b, where Mall is the full set of models in the dataset. A model is considered to participate in b if it 176 reports a valid score, regardless of the score magnitude.
[130] [Normal] 177 To avoid circularity, we use the LOBO-calibrated capability score defined in Section 3.2, which 178 is computed by excluding benchmark b from the capability estimation process. The raw capability 179 coverage of benchmark b is defined as:

[132] [Normal] 181 The square-root mapping compresses the upper range of the coverage ratio, preventing highly reported 182 benchmarks from dominating solely due to participation volume, while still preserving the advantage 183 of benchmarks evaluated by high-capability models.
[133] [Normal] 3.4.2	Interpretation
[134] [Normal] This formulation captures impact as capability-weighted adoption. A benchmark receives a high 186 SImp when a substantial portion of the ecosystem’s calibrated model capability is represented among 187 its evaluated participants. Because the capability scores are computed using LOBO calibration, the 188 benchmark being assessed does not contribute to the capability estimates used to measure its own 189 impact.
[135] [Normal] By construction, SImp is bounded within [0,1] and does not depend on benchmark release dates,
[136] [Normal] model release dates, community metadata, or heuristic temporal decay. This design makes the impact 192 score less sensitive to raw popularity accumulation and better aligned with the technical adoption of 193 benchmarks by capable models.

[143] [Normal] 5.1	Main BHI Results
[144] [Normal] The final BHI is synthesized from the three primary dimensions introduced in Section 3: SDisc,
[145] [Normal] SAS, and SImp. In this process, the SAS and SImp axes are adjusted by the Model Capability 209 Calibration component—derived from the LOBO-adjusted win rate—to transform heterogeneous 210 benchmark-level signals into a unified health score.
[146] [Normal] 211 We utilize the CRITIC objective weighting framework, as detailed in Appendix F, for the final index 212 aggregation to maintain a consistent data-driven formulation. This method determines the importance
[147] [Normal] of each dimension by evaluating its internal information volume, measured by contrast intensity,
[148] [Normal] and its degree of independence, measured by conflicting character, within the global distribution of 215 158 benchmarks. Under this framework, the optimized weighting distribution is 32.63% for SDisc, 216 35.74% for SAS, and 31.63% for SImp, resulting in the final formulation:
[149] [Normal] BHI(b) = wDisc · SDisc(b) + wAS · SAS(b) + wImp · SImp(b)	(11)

[197] [Heading 1] 306 7	Conclusion
[198] [Normal] This paper introduces the Benchmark Health Index (BHI), a data-driven framework for auditing
[199] [Normal] LLM benchmarks at the ecosystem level. Unlike conventional model-centric leaderboards, BHI treats
[200] [Normal] benchmarks themselves as objects of evaluation and quantifies their health through discrimination, 310 anti-saturation, and capability-weighted adoption. Based on large-scale public technical reports, 311 BHI produces interpretable benchmark rankings and reveals current failure modes in the evaluation 312 ecosystem. Validation further shows that its components are necessary, complementary, and stable. 313 Overall, BHI provides a reproducible quantitative basis for benchmark selection, lifecycle monitoring, 314 and systematic benchmark governance.

[469] [Heading 1] 921 A	Limitations
[470] [Normal] BHI is, to the best of our knowledge, the first benchmark-level auditing framework that systematically
[471] [Normal] quantifies benchmark health across discrimination, anti-saturation, and ecosystem impact, rather than 924 an absolute measure of model capability or benchmark quality. Its conclusions should therefore be 925 interpreted as relative diagnostic signals within the collected evaluation ecosystem. The framework is 926 most informative when a benchmark has sufficient public reporting coverage and when the reported 927 scores are comparable after normalization. Benchmarks with limited adoption, highly specialized 928 evaluation protocols, or rapidly changing versions may receive less stable estimates, even though the 929 LOBO calibration in Section 3.2 and the coverage analysis in Appendix I are designed to reduce this 930 effect.
[472] [Normal] 931 A second limitation comes from the heterogeneity of public technical reports. Although the data 932 pipeline in Section 3 and Appendix E applies systematic filtering, score normalization, and benchmark
[473] [Normal] alignment, model reports do not always disclose evaluation prompts, decoding settings, test splits,
[474] [Normal] contamination controls, or metric variants with the same level of detail. As a result, BHI should not 935 be viewed as replacing benchmark-specific replication studies. Instead, it provides a reproducible 936 macro-level audit that can help identify where such more detailed studies are most needed.
[475] [Normal] 937 The anti-saturation component also relies on several simplifying assumptions. In Section 3.3, we 938 model short-term score evolution using a linear trend and a fixed projection window. This choice
[476] [Normal] 939 is intentionally conservative and is supported by the sensitivity analyses in Appendix H, but real 940 benchmark lifecycles may be nonlinear: a benchmark can experience abrupt saturation after a new 941 training recipe, tool-use capability, or data exposure event becomes widespread. Therefore, the 942 dynamic component should be interpreted as a near-term warning signal rather than a long-horizon 943 forecast.
[477] [Normal] 944 The impact score in Section 3.4 measures capability-weighted adoption among models with valid 945 public reports. This design avoids relying on raw popularity signals such as citations or repository
[478] [Normal] statistics, but it also means that BHI captures reported evaluation practice rather than all forms of
[479] [Normal] scientific influence. For example, a benchmark may shape research discussions, diagnostic analysis,
[480] [Normal] or internal evaluation pipelines without appearing frequently in public model reports. Conversely, a 949 widely reported benchmark may retain high adoption even after its technical diagnostic value has 950 begun to decline. The three-axis decomposition is intended to expose this tension, but it cannot fully 951 observe private or undocumented evaluation use.
[481] [Normal] 952 Our external validation is also necessarily limited. The case study in Table 3 shows that BHI separates 953 independently identified saturated benchmarks from active frontier benchmarks, providing evidence 954 of directional consistency with current evaluation practice. However, this should be understood as 955 face validity rather than criterion validity against an independently collected ground-truth health score.
[482] [Normal] 956 Building such a ground truth would require structured expert elicitation or longitudinal replication 957 studies, which we leave for future work.
[483] [Normal] Finally, BHI is a lightweight post-hoc auditing procedure, but its scalability still depends on the
[484] [Normal] availability and maintenance of structured benchmark metadata. The computational cost of the 960 index itself is modest compared with model evaluation, since it operates on reported scores rather 961 than rerunning benchmarks. The main cost lies in data curation, version alignment, and continuous 962 updates as new models and benchmarks appear. Future work could integrate BHI with existing agent 963 frameworks to support automated benchmark report parsing, metadata alignment, and periodic index 964 updates as new models and benchmarks emerge.
[485] [Heading 1] 965 B	Expanded Related Work
[486] [Normal] B.1	Shifting Evaluation Paradigms
[487] [Normal] The meteoric rise of LLMs has catalyzed a paradigm shift in evaluation, moving from single-task 968 leaderboards toward comprehensive benchmark suites that encompass knowledge, reasoning, and 969 safety [1, 4]. However, conventional evaluation frameworks predominantly adopt a model-centric 970 perspective, treating benchmarks as static and immutable measurement tools. As model capabilities 971 continue to leapfrog, public benchmarks are increasingly susceptible to score inflation and leaderboard 972 distortion, which diminish their capacity to characterize genuine differences in model performance [8]. 973 Consequently, the research focus in the community is gradually pivoting from simple performance
[488] [Normal] 974 comparisons between models to in-depth investigations into the validity, robustness, and lifecycles 975 of the benchmarks themselves [39, 15, 19]. Despite this shift, existing studies typically propose 976 diagnostic metrics centered on local dimensions—such as uncertainty, measurement quality, or 977 cross-benchmark consistency—and lack a unified, scalable framework for the longitudinal monitoring 978 of benchmark efficacy as it co-evolves with model advancements.
[489] [Normal] B.2	Reliability and Measurement Theory
[490] [Normal] The reliability of evaluation outcomes faces severe challenges from prompt sensitivity, annotation 981 noise, and stochasticity in sampling [40, 41]. To mitigate these issues, researchers have integrated
[491] [Normal] statistical tools such as bootstrap estimation, confidence intervals, and significance testing to distin-
[492] [Normal] guish true performance gains from measurement noise, establishing more robust reporting norms 984 [42]. From the perspectives of psychometrics and Item Response Theory (IRT), the core utility 985 of a benchmark lies in its discriminative power—the measurement resolution jointly determined
[493] [Normal] 986 by item difficulty and discrimination parameters [43]. In the context of LLM evaluation, however, 987 high-performing models often trigger upper-tail compression and a lack of leaderboard separability, 988 rendering traditional metrics ineffective at identifying fine-grained capability nuances.
[494] [Normal] B.3	Benchmark Degradation and Dynamics
[495] [Normal] The efficacy of evaluation benchmarks exhibits a discernible degradation trend over time, primar-
[496] [Normal] ily driven by saturation effects and data contamination [17, 44]. When models approach ceiling 992 performance on existing datasets, or when evaluation data is implicitly leaked into pre-training 993 corpora, the discriminative capacity of a benchmark shrinks rapidly. This blurs the boundary between
[497] [Normal] generalization and memorization, prompting the development of contamination detection and veri-
[498] [Normal] fiable evaluation corrections [16, 45]. To address the loss of resolution caused by saturation, some 996 work attempts to reactivate the discriminative power of existing benchmarks through weighted or 997 augmented metrics, thus improving the separability among SOTA models [46]. Although dynamic
[499] [Normal] evaluation—which refreshes the task pools or introduces temporal windows—can alleviate contami-
[500] [Normal] nation risks, it introduces a trade-off between maintaining longitudinal comparability and controlling 1000 evaluation costs [6, 47]. Therefore, a significant research gap remains in the construction of a unified 1001 data-driven framework for the long-term tracking of benchmark health [39, 17, 48].
